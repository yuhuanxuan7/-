Generally, each country has its own native sign language that is similar to their spoken counterpart, however the grammar and sentence structure may vary to encourage efficiency and fluidity. This is further complicated by regional variations, similar to the regional dialects found in spoken languages. In addition, only some are formally recognised. Therefore, it is unclear how many sign languages exist worldwide. In the 2017 edition of Ethnologue they only list 142 sign languages.


While we are unable to list all variations of sign language, linguistics may classify sign languages by how they arise:


  • Home sign (languages) arise in non-signing communities where the deaf individual is not educated in sign and lacks contact with other deaf individuals. The result is therefore not a full language, but closer to a pidgin
  • Village sign languages arise over several generations in a relatively insular community with a high incidence of deafness, and is used both by the deaf and by a significant portion of the hearing community, who have deaf family and friends
  • Deaf-community sign languages arise where deaf people come together to form their own communities
  • Speech-taboo languages contrast with both languages as they are developed by the hearing community and used secondarily by the deaf

In addition, while it may be difficult, due to language contact and evolution, we can classify sign languages into families (see Figure on The Classification of Sign Language Families).
